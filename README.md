# AIAA DBF 2026: What I Learned as Team Lead & Chief Engineer

## Overview:
I led a team of 6 to design, build, and fly an aircraft that carried 3 ducks and 1 hockey puck, deployed a 4-meter banner, and competed against 97 universities worldwide, ranked 28th in report round and 32nd at the fly-off. We are a group of more than 30 people where we focus on aircraft design, knowledge sharing, building and flying. 

Here are what I learned when things went wrong and right in this 9-month project.

---

## Who I Am (The Short Version)

I'm a mechanical engineering student who:
- **Loves complex systems** — the kind where numerous variables and constraints are to be considered, and one wrong decision ripples through everything.
- **Thrives under pressure** — I discovered that I think clearest even if everyone else is panicking in stressful situation. It made me blast out my energy knowing that I'm tackling a difficult challenge.
- **Takes ownership** — when someone said “we can't finish the Technical Inspection on day 1”, "We can't do prepare well before flight missions" I ignored him and kept working. We finished it.

I'm not the loudest person in the room. But I'm the one who:
- Catches the rule change everyone missed
- Builds the framework so no one has to “remember” anything
- Takes the blame when my team fails — and gives the credit when they succeed

## The One Thing I Will Always Remind Myself

We had perfect test flights. Everyone was confident. Then Mission 3 failed — and I lost my confidence for weeks.

I later realized: the failure wasn't in our design. It was in our **system**.

I had built a team that relied on a few, but not redundancy. I didn't have AB Corner System. But most importantly, I forgot our own pressure test because it wasn't built into our habits, which were the biggest reason that led to our failure in Mission 3.

This is the most painful yet valuable lesson for me in this project, but it will forever remind me in all my future projects.
